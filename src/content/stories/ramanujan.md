He was working as a clerk at the Madras Port Trust, earning a modest salary, when he posted a letter to a Cambridge professor he had never met. Inside were around a hundred mathematical statements, written without proof, in handwriting that gave no hint of who had produced them.

G. H. Hardy nearly threw it away. Then he read it again, and later said the theorems "must be true, because, if they were not true, no one would have had the imagination to invent them."

## Before Cambridge

Srinivasa Ramanujan was born in Erode in 1887 and grew up in Kumbakonam. At fifteen he came across a book of some five thousand mathematical results — a dry compendium, no explanations — and worked through it alone. That book, more or less, was his mathematical education.

It made him extraordinary at mathematics and useless at everything else. He failed college twice, not from any lack of ability but because he would not study anything that wasn't mathematics. He spent his twenties poor, unemployed, and unknown, filling notebooks with results in the margins and on a slate he wiped clean to save paper.

## The Cambridge years

In 1914 Ramanujan sailed to England. The collaboration with Hardy produced work on partitions, continued fractions, and infinite series that reshaped whole areas of number theory. Hardy, an atheist and a formalist, spent years teaching Ramanujan the proofs his intuition kept skipping over. It was, by Hardy's own account, the one romantic incident in his life.

England did not suit him. He was a strict vegetarian in a country at war and short on food, isolated by cold, illness, and distance from home. He was elected a Fellow of the Royal Society at thirty-one — among the youngest ever — and returned to India the following year, already dying.

He was thirty-two when he died in 1920.

## What he left

Around 3,900 results, most stated without proof. The last of them — the "lost notebook," recovered in 1976 — described mock theta functions that mathematicians would not fully understand for another sixty years. Some of it now turns up in the study of black holes.

He never explained where any of it came from. Asked, he said the goddess Namagiri showed him the formulas in dreams.
