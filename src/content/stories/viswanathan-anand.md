My mother taught me chess when I was six, mostly to keep me occupied during long train journeys. I had no idea then that a board with sixty-four squares would end up taking me around the world.

Chennai in those years didn't have the chess infrastructure bigger cities abroad did — no army of coaches, no deep tournament circuit. What we had instead was patience, a strong local chess culture, and people willing to drive me to every tournament that could get me stronger.

Winning the World Championship, and defending it more than once, still feels less important to me than what came after — watching chess grow in India, watching kids from towns much smaller than Chennai pick up the game because they believed, for the first time, that someone from here could actually become world champion.
